on finally ricing my setup (sort of)

I never actually riced my Arch Linux setup, but I always wanted to. Now that AI exists, it was relatively facile to vibe rice my website.

The Confession

For years, I ran Arch Linux with i3wm. You know the type — tiling window manager, terminal-everything, the works. But here's the thing: I never actually riced it.

My setup was functional but painfully stock. No custom colors, no fancy ASCII art in my terminal, no painstakingly curated .dotfiles repo with 47 stars on GitHub. Just... default everything. The aesthetic equivalent of wearing cargo shorts to a hackathon.

I always wanted to. Spent hours browsing r/unixporn, saving screenshots of people's beautiful terminals with their perfectly coordinated color schemes and elegant status bars. But the activation energy was too high. Learning to configure everything, tweaking colors one by one, debugging why your terminal emulator won't render that specific shade of cyan... it never happened.

Enter AI

Then LLMs happened. And suddenly the idea of "vibe ricing" became possible. You know — just describe what you want, and let Claude figure out the CSS, the layout, the terminal colors.

So instead of ricing my actual Linux setup (which I don't even use anymore — I'm on a Mac now, the ultimate betrayal), I riced this website instead.

An i3wm-inspired portfolio site. Terminal aesthetics. ASCII art. Status bar with fake system stats. The whole aesthetic I always wanted but never actually built.

The Irony

The funny part? This website is my dotfiles now. Instead of a carefully maintained repo of Vim configs and i3 status bar scripts, I have a Next.js site with Tailwind CSS that looks like i3wm.

It's meta. It's probably a little cringe. But honestly? It's more me than any actual rice would have been. Because this is what I actually use — websites, not window managers.

The Process

The whole thing took an afternoon with Claude Code. The hardest part wasn't the code — it was deciding what I actually wanted. Turns out "make it look cool" isn't a very good prompt.

But once I figured out the vibe (dark terminal colors, green accents, split-screen layout, ASCII phage for flair), Claude just... built it. Workspace switching, responsive mobile layout, the whole thing.

Would I recommend this approach for actual dotfiles? Probably not. But for a personal website that captures the aesthetic you always wanted but never built? Absolutely.

Why This Matters

There's something interesting here about how AI changes the accessibility of aesthetics. Ricing used to be a rite of passage — proof you'd put in the hours to customize every detail. Now you can skip straight to the result.

Is that good? Bad? I don't know. But it's definitely different.

What I do know is that I finally have the terminal aesthetic I always wanted. It just happens to be in a browser instead of an actual terminal.

Close enough.

P.S. — Yes, I know real i3 doesn't have ASCII art terminals. That's not the point. The point is the vibe.
